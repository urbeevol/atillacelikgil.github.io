My research lies at the intersection of evolutionary ecology and global change biology, driven by a fascination with the dynamic interplay between ecological and evolutionary processes. I am particularly interested in how these forces jointly shape organismal adaptation, species interactions, and the generation and maintenance of biodiversity, especially in the context of anthropogenic stressors.

RESEARCH TOPICS
======
I am an evolutionary ecologist investigating how global environmental change reshapes biodiversity across genomic, species, and ecosystem levels. By integrating evolutionary ecology, population genomics, and spatiotemporal modelling, my research aims to uncover how human-driven environmental change alters biodiversity, species interactions, and ecosystem functioning to better understand and anticipate biodiversity responses in the Anthropocene.

My research is broadly situated within community and evolutionary ecology of insects, with a particular focus on pollinators. To this end, I combine molecular approaches (e.g., whole-genome resequencing, metabarcoding of pollen) with high-resolution observational studies and spatially explicit models. A central thread of my work examines how anthropogenic stressors—such as urban intensification, and urban heat effects drive genomic, demographic and community level changes in wild pollinator populations.
